Disaster Response

What to do after a disaster strikes

After a Flood

Floodwaters can be dangerous and contaminated. Your first priority is to get to safety.

If You Are Alone

Focus on your immediate safety and staying informed.

  • Move to the highest ground possible and wait for rescuers.
  • Do not walk or drive through moving floodwaters. The water can be deeper and faster than it appears.
  • Avoid contact with floodwater as it may be contaminated with sewage or hide sharp objects.
  • Listen to a battery-powered radio for official evacuation orders and updates.

If Others Are Injured

Your safety comes first; do not attempt a rescue that puts you in danger.

  • Contact the NDRF, SDRF, or local emergency services with your location and the number of people affected.
  • Help others move to higher ground if it is safe to do so.
  • Do not enter fast-moving water to rescue someone unless you are a trained professional.
  • Offer reassurance and help keep victims calm and warm while waiting for help.

After an Earthquake

The danger is not over when the shaking stops. Aftershocks are a major risk.

If You Are Alone

Carefully assess your situation and surroundings.

  • Be prepared for aftershocks.If they happen, Drop, Cover, and Hold On.
  • Check yourself for injuries first before moving.
  • If it is safe, move to an open area away from damaged buildings, walls, and power lines.
  • If you suspect a gas leak or electrical damage, turn off the mains if you can do so safely.

If Others Are Injured

Help where you can without becoming a victim yourself.

  • Check on others and provide basic first aid if you are trained and it is safe.
  • Do not move seriously injured people unless they are in immediate danger of further injury.
  • Contact emergency services and report the number of injured people and their conditions.
  • Help extinguish small fires if possible, but evacuate immediately in case of a large fire.

After a Landslide

The immediate area of a landslide remains extremely dangerous.

If You Are Alone

Your priority is to get away from the unstable area.

  • Stay clear of the slide zone, as secondary slides and debris flows are common.
  • Check yourself for injuries and move to stable, higher ground.
  • Avoid broken utility lines and be alert for other dangers like fallen trees or rocks.
  • Do not return to the area until authorities have declared it safe.

If Others Are Injured

Do not enter the slide zone. Your best course of action is to get help from professionals.

  • Contact the NDRF/SDRF or local police immediately. Provide the exact location and number of people trapped or injured.
  • Do not attempt to dig people out yourself, as this can cause further collapses.
  • From a safe distance, try to direct rescue teams to the location of victims.

After a Forest Fire

Even after the main fire passes, the area can be hazardous.

If You Are Alone

Ensure you are completely clear of danger and let people know you are safe.

  • Move to a designated safe meeting point or a large, clear area with no vegetation.
  • Be aware of hot spots or smoldering embers that could flare up again.
  • Watch out for fire-weakened trees or branches that could fall.
  • Once you are safe, contact family or emergency services to let them know your status.

If Others Are Injured

Getting everyone out of the smoke and heat is the top priority.

  • Help guide people away from the fire towards a safe, open area.
  • If someone has minor burns, cool the area with clean water. For serious burns, call for immediate medical help.
  • For smoke inhalation, move the person to fresh air and help them stay calm.
  • Contact the forest department or fire services to report injuries and your location.
