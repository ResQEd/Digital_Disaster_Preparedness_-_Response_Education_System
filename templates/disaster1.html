Introduction to Floods in India

Floods in India are powerful, recurring phenomena mainly triggered by the South-West Monsoon. They cause the overflowing of rivers and streams, flooding land that is usually dry and leading to loss of life, disruption to livelihood, and big economic setbacks.

Causes and Effects
Natural Causes
  • Heavy Monsoon Rain
  • River Siltation
  • Cloudbursts & Cyclones
Human Causes
  • Encroachment on floodplains
  • Poor Urban Drainage
  • Deforestation
Effects

Impacts include loss of life, economic damage (over ₹1,805 crore yearly), health crises from waterborne diseases, and severe damage to infrastructure and agriculture.

Management and Rescue

Management is handled by agencies like NDMA, NDRF, and the Central Water Commission. Rescue efforts involve evacuation, setting up shelters, and providing emergency supplies and financial relief to victims.


Introduction to Earthquakes in India

An earthquake is a sudden, violent shaking of the ground caused by a rapid release of energy in the Earth's crust. Nearly 59% of India's land is vulnerable to tremors due to the Indian tectonic plate pushing into the Eurasian plate.

Causes and Types

The main cause is Plate Tectonics. Human-induced causes include large dams (Reservoir-Induced Seismicity) and mining. The most common and destructive are Tectonic Earthquakes, especially shallow ones which cause more surface damage.


India's Seismic Zones

India is divided into four zones:

  • Zone V (Very High Risk): Northeast India, Kashmir, Rann of Kutch.
  • Zone IV (High Risk): Delhi, North Bihar, Indo-Gangetic Plains.
  • Zone III (Moderate Risk): Chennai, Mumbai, Kolkata.
  • Zone II (Low Risk): Parts of Deccan Plateau.

Safety Measures (Drop, Cover, Hold)
Before:

Secure heavy furniture and prepare an emergency kit.

During:

DROP to the floor, take COVER under a sturdy table, and HOLD ON until shaking stops.

After:

Expect aftershocks, check for injuries, and stay away from damaged buildings.

Introduction to Forest Fires in India

A forest fire is an uncontrolled blaze spreading through vegetation, most often occurring in India's hot, dry summer months (March–June). Causes can be natural (lightning) or human (burning waste, careless smoking).

Types of Forest Fires

  • Surface Fires: Burn along the ground through leaf litter. Common in central and southern India.
  • Crown Fires: Intense fires that jump between treetops. Occur in dense Himalayan forests.
  • Ground Fires: Slow, smoldering fires below the surface. Found in peat-rich forests of the North-East.

Prone Areas and Effects

High-risk areas include the Himalayan foothills, Central India, and the Western Ghats. Effects include loss of biodiversity, soil damage, severe air pollution, and economic losses from timber and tourism.

Prevention and Safety

Prevention methods include creating cleared 'fire lines,' running awareness programs, and using satellite monitoring. For personal safety, avoid ignition sources and follow local alerts. Response is managed by agencies like the Forest Survey of India (FSI) and State Forest Departments.

Introduction to Landslides in India

A landslide is the movement of rock, debris, or earth down a slope, common in India’s Himalayan and Western Ghats regions. They are caused by heavy rain, earthquakes, deforestation, and unscientific construction.

Types and Effects

Types range from rapid rockfalls and debris flows to very slow 'creep'. Effects include loss of life and property, river blockages that risk flash floods, and major disruption to transport routes.

Safety and Prevention
Before:

Know your area's risk and plan evacuation routes. Watch for warning signs like new cracks in the ground or tilted trees.

During:

Evacuate immediately. Move away from the slide path to higher ground.

After:

Stay clear of the slide zone and report injuries. Prevention involves engineering solutions like retaining walls and planning measures like hazard zonation mapping.
